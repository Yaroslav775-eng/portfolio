Finding the Google Ads Scaling Ceiling for a Pet Supplies Store in Ukraine.

The Problem: Scaling Hit Demand Quality, Not Just Budget

At the start, the account did not suffer from a lack of demand. The harder problem was that increasing reach pushed the system into broader audiences, and that did not create proportional order growth. Some traffic became less qualified, and the economics became less stable.

Search, Shopping and Performance Max were also able to overlap. Search helped collect initial demand at the beginning, but over time it started to cannibalize demand together with Shopping and PMax. The solution was not to simply increase spend, but to understand where new demand came from, where repeated touchpoints mattered, where returning customers should be excluded, and where ads were taking credit for sales that were already likely to happen.

Key Insight

We did not chase maximum reach. After several iterations, it became clear that the best result came from an optimal working point: controlled reach, correct product priorities, returning customer exclusions and demand support through repeated audience touchpoints.

What Changed

The work lasted 6 months and was not one large switch. It was a sequence of iterations: first make demand easier to read, then separate returning customers and cannibalization, and only then scale the categories where the economics held up.

The New Working Model

Instead of expanding in every direction, the account was rebuilt around clear roles: which campaigns collect ready demand, which support product categories, and which remind audiences about the brand and promotions.

  • Product prioritization. A catalog of roughly 2,500 SKUs was reviewed through demand, margin, promotion logic and scaling potential.
  • Demand isolation. Returning customers were excluded from part of the audiences to avoid mixing repeat purchases with new demand and overstating efficiency.
  • Shift from Search to product-led demand. Search was useful at the start, but after cannibalization became visible, it was removed from the active structure. The account focused on Performance Max, Shopping campaigns and DSA.
  • Feed and product card optimization. Product titles were rewritten around search intent so the system could better match products to relevant queries.
  • Promotions, banners and video. New static banners, video creatives and promo campaigns were launched to support category demand and keep reminding audiences about the brand.
  • Trust signals on product cards. Product reviews were added to improve ratings and make product cards more convincing before purchase.

Demand Creation and Demand Support

A separate part of the work was not about immediate sales, but about a longer cycle of reminding the audience. Static banners, video, promotions and repeated touchpoints helped support interest in categories that are not always purchased after the first interaction.

As a result, Google Ads stopped being only a tool for harvesting existing demand. It became a system with several roles: product prioritization, demand support, remarketing, cannibalization control and product feed improvement.

The Outcome: Not Maximum Reach, but a Better Working Model

After several iterations, it became clear that uncontrolled reach expansion was not the best path. The best result came from a balanced model: priority products, cleaner demand, returning customer exclusions, audience support through promos and creatives, and control over where campaigns started to cannibalize each other.

In this model, average CPA stabilized at €4.35, ROAS held in the 800–1200% range, and the account tracked 34,923 conversions during the analyzed period.

The main result was not only the final metrics. The business gained a clearer answer to which categories could be scaled, where demand needed support, and where expanding the audience started to damage the economics.

What We Learned

This stage mattered not only because of the final metrics, but because it clarified the scaling ceiling and the role of new tools in the niche.

  • Every niche has a scaling ceiling. More reach does not always mean more qualified orders. After a certain point, broader audiences can make the economics worse.
  • The first question is whether demand already exists. If demand exists, the first job is to collect it correctly, separate it, and avoid mixing it with repeat purchases or branded touchpoints.
  • When ready demand is exhausted, demand has to be created. This is why promotions, video, banners, repeated touchpoints and product card improvements became part of the system, not decoration.
  • Steady growth needs planning. Scaling has to be tied to budgeting, product priorities, feed control, audience logic and iterative testing, not just higher daily spend.
