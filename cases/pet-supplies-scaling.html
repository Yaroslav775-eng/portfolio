The "Black Box" Problem

For an e-commerce store with thousands of SKUs (like Pet Supplies), the default Google Ads setup often becomes a financial black box. The client had dozens of campaigns running simultaneously. Budget was being spent at scale, but nobody could definitively explain which specific campaigns were generating profit, and which were quietly cannibalizing the budget.

They were trapped in a cycle of "spending money to make money" without knowing the actual levers of their growth.

The Technical Intervention

We tore down the convoluted setup and restructured the account from scratch around a fundamental principle of performance marketing: Every campaign must have one specific job.

The 3-Layer Fenced Architecture

To stop campaigns from bidding against each other for the same users, we implemented strict negative keyword boundaries and audience signals.

  • Layer 1: High-Intent Search. Captured users specifically searching for brand names or exact product matches. Ready to buy.
  • Layer 2: Segmented Shopping. Handled broad catalog visibility. We split "Dogs" and "Cats" into separate clusters so budget could follow real market demand, rather than being averaged across the whole assortment.
  • Layer 3: Fenced Performance Max. Tasked purely with incremental reach. PMax was strictly prevented from cannibalizing Brand terms, ensuring its reported ROAS was genuinely new revenue, not stolen credit.

The Outcome: Scalability

Once each layer of the account knew its specific role—and couldn't overlap with the others—optimization became mathematical rather than emotional. ROAS stabilized within the target 800-1200% range, and the Average CPA dropped to just €4.35.

More importantly, the business owner regained control. When scaling is required, they now know exactly which lever to pull without risking the bottom line.
